Research Experience

Harvard Seismology Group

Undergraduate Research Position

The Story

During the spring of 2020, I took an introductory seismology class, more out of curiosity than anything else. A few weeks before Harvard sent all undergraduates home, my professor, Marine A. Denolle mentioned that she had a few opportunties doing research with her group. I jumped at the chance and when my original summer plans were cancelled, I was able to stay on for the summer. From group meetings and presentations, to outside talks and question sessions, to debugging code and most recently submitting an abstract for AGU this fall, I've gotten to experience what working on, and for the most part leading, a long-term research project is like. The current plan is to continue research into the fall and write several papers about our project and results (see below).

The Details:

The project I'm working on involves using seismic data from California to produce large-scale ambient noise cross-correlations (ANCC), with the goal of running these correlations for a 20 year period. These cross-correlations are mostly used for tomography and monitoring to, for example, study groundwater level changes, map velocities of seismic waves, and predict groundmotion in an earthquake. As the number of cross-correlations we need to compute is so large, I've learned the high-performance computing language Julia (no, sadly it's not named after me) as well as the interfaces for Amazon's EC2 and S3 cloud computing and storage interfaces, as we expect to process several terabytes of data. While my focus has been building the codes and infrastructure to run the cross-correlations, we've been working with seismologists at Stanford and San Diego State Universities to expand our correlation network to the whole of California, and cross-validate a Los Angeles earthquake simulation with the ANCCs, respectively. The abstract to the paper I'm working on this fall, along with my professor, a graduate student, and the Stanford team, can be found above.
